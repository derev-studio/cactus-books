The first steps - from curiosity to science: The birth of interest in the thorny world

Christopher Columbus saw cacti.

The history of cacti in Europe dates back to the Age of Discovery. Christopher Columbus, who set foot in the New World in 1492, was the first European to see these unusual plants. In his journals, he mentioned &quot;strange trees without leaves, covered with thorns,&quot; which the locals used for various purposes, such as building fences and eating. These entries, although they did not contain detailed botanical descriptions, aroused great interest in Europe and gave rise to many legends and myths about strange plants from across the ocean.

First images and inaccuracies:

In the sixteenth century, with the development of printing, the first illustrated books about plants, herbalists (herbaria), began to appear. In them, you can find images of cacti, but due to the lack of information and inaccuracies in the descriptions, these images were often far from reality. Cacti were depicted with leaves, flowers that were not typical of them, and even with fruits that resembled European fruits. An interesting fact: because of their unusual appearance, cacti were often associated with mythical creatures and monsters. In some early engravings, they were depicted as spiny monsters, which, of course, did not contribute to the scientific understanding of these plants.

Among the authors who attempted to describe cacti during this period are Matthias de L'Obel (1538-1616): This Flemish botanist, court physician to William of Orange, is known for his work on plant classification. In his works, such as Stirpium Adversaria Nova (1571), there are images of plants that are identified today as cacti, albeit under other, often fantastic names. For example, he described plants that looked like artichokes covered with thorns.

Pierre Pena (Pierre Repa) (c. 1535-1605): French botanist who collaborated with Nobel. Together, they created the aforementioned Stirpium Adversaria Nova. Their work was important because it contained not only descriptions but also engravings, which allowed us to better visualize what these exotic plants looked like.

A curiosity: because cacti were often brought to Europe already dried, the first botanists often had difficulty identifying them. Dried plants lost their shape and color, which led to errors in descriptions.

The eighteenth century was marked by the emergence of Carl Linnaeus (1707-1778), a Swedish physician and naturalist whose work had a huge impact on the development of biology. 1753.

Linnaeus published his main work Species Plantarum, in which he proposed a binary nomenclature, a system of double names for each species, consisting of a genus and a species name. This system is still used today and is the basis of modern biological classification.

In his Species Plantarum, Linnaeus identified the genus Cactus, uniting under this name all known succulents! plants with thorns, except for prickly pears, which he assigned to a separate genus Opuntia. This was the first step towards the scientific classification of cacti and put an end to the confusion in their names.

An interesting fact: Linnaeus, although he laid the foundations for the classification of cacti, had never seen them in their natural habitat. His knowledge was based on descriptions and specimens brought by other researchers.

From Linnaeus to the first specialized works:

After Linnaeus, interest in cacti continued to grow, and in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, the first monographs devoted exclusively to this plant family appeared. These works, such as Adrian Haworth's Histoire des Cactees (1813), contained more detailed descriptions and illustrations, and took into account new discoveries.

Curiosity: at that time there were many disputes about which family cacti belonged to. they were either classified as euphorbiaceae or purslaneae, and only further research made it possible to distinguish them into a separate family Cactaceae.

This period was a transition from mere curiosity and description of curiosities to the scientific study of cacti. It paved the way for the emergence of such prominent scientists as Wilhelm Friedrich Karwinski, who made a huge contribution to the study of cacti in their natural habitat. History of Ariocarpus kotschoubeyanus

The history of botanical discoveries is often intertwined with the fates of different people: brave travelers, generous patrons of the arts, and painstaking scientists. The story of Ariocarpus kotschoubeyanus is a vivid example of this. It closely intertwines the fates of Baron Wilhelm Friedrich Karwinsky, Prince Vasily Viktorovich Kochubey, and, of course, this amazing cactus itself.

Karwinski (1780-1855) was not just a lover of exotic plants. He was a Bavarian physician and an accomplished botanist who worked on behalf of the Russian government. His mission was to collect plant specimens for the Imperial Botanical Garden in St. Petersburg. In the early 1840s, he traveled to Mexico, a country full of botanical treasures.

Prince Vasily Viktorovich Kochubey

These expeditions were made possible by the generous financial support of Prince Vasyl Viktorovych Kochubey (1812-1850). The prince, who came from a noble Russian family, showed a keen interest in natural history and understood the importance of scientific research. It was thanks to his investments that Karwinski was able to undertake his travels, which were full of dangers and difficulties.

Now about the cactus itself. Ariocarpus kotschoubeyanus is an unusual plant that resembles a gray stone rather than a flowering cactus. It was scientifically described in 1839 by the French botanist Charles Nomer. It is important to emphasize that Karwinski did not personally describe this species.

However, there is a connection between Karwinski and this cactus, and it is very important. The species epithet kotschoubeyanus was given by Nomer in honor of Prince Kochubey, the same patron who financed Karwinski's expeditions. Thus, the name of the cactus immortalized the memory of the man who made it possible for Karwinski to contribute to the study of Mexican flora.

Karwinski, traveling in Mexico in 1840-1843, certainly visited the same regions where Ariocarpus kotschoubeyanus grows. It is likely that he saw this cactus with his own eyes and may have even collected specimens. These specimens could have found their way into European collections and contributed to the further study of this and other species.

Karwinski's contribution to botany is not in the description of specific species, but in the large-scale collection and documentation of the plant life of Mexico. He provided scientists with a wealth of research material that is still valuable today. Mammillaria karwinskiana, for example, is named in his honor, which is another testament to his merits.

Alexander von Humboldt was born on September 14, 1769, in Berlin, into an aristocratic family. Having received an excellent education, he showed an interest in the natural sciences from a young age. Traveling in America (1799-1804): the main achievement.

The most famous and significant event in Humboldt's life was his five-year expedition to America, which he made together with the French botanist Aimé Bonplan. This journey was a real scientific revolution.

The route of the expedition:

Humboldt and Bonplan visited the Canary Islands, Venezuela, Cuba, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and Mexico. They explored the Orinoco and Amazon rivers, climbed the Andes Mountains, studied volcanoes, and collected huge collections of plants, animals, and minerals.

Research methods: Humboldt took a comprehensive approach to the study of nature. He measured the height of mountains, air and water temperature, atmospheric pressure, studied geology, climate, vegetation, and fauna. He sought to understand the relationships between all natural phenomena.

The results of the expedition:

The results of the expedition were stunning. Humboldt collected thousands of plant specimens, many of which were described for the first time. He made detailed maps, described climatic zones, studied the effect of altitude on vegetation, and discovered the Humboldt Current off the coast of South America.

Humboldt's contribution to science:

Humboldt's contribution to science is enormous and multifaceted. He:

He laid the foundations of phytogeography: He established the link between climate and plant distribution, creating the science of the geographical distribution of vegetation.

He developed comparative climatology: He studied the climate of different regions of the world and discovered patterns in the distribution of climatic zones.

He made a contribution to geology and volcanology: He studied the geological structure of different regions, investigated volcanoes and described their eruptions.

He promoted the development of geography as a science: He viewed geography as a complex science that studies the relationships between various natural phenomena.

He popularized science: Humboldt was an excellent writer and lecturer. His books, such as Journey to the Equatorial Regions of the New World and Cosmos, were very popular and contributed to the spread of scientific knowledge.

The influence of Humboldt:

Humboldt had a huge impact on the development of science and culture. His ideas inspired many scientists, writers and travelers, including Charles Darwin, Henry David Thoreau and many others. Streets, universities, mountains, rivers, and even a lunar crater are named after him.

It is worth highlighting a few key figures:

Carl Moritz Schumann (1851-1904): German botanist, curator of the Botanical Garden in Berlin. Schumann is considered one of the founders of modern cactus taxonomy. His work &quot;Gesamtbeschreibung der Kakteen&quot; (1897-1899) was a fundamental work in which he described and classified all known species of cacti at that time. Schumann's classification had a huge impact on subsequent researchers, including Bakeberg.

Nathaniel Lord Britton (1859-1934) and Joseph Nelson Rose (1862-1928): American botanists, authors of the monumental four-volume work The Cactaceae (1919-1923). This work, based on extensive field research and study of herbarium materials, became an important milestone in the study of cacti in the New World. Britton and Rose's classification also had a significant impact on Bakeberg.

How the systematics of cacti has changed:

The evolution of cactus taxonomy can be divided into several stages:

Early descriptions and classifications (XVIII-XIX centuries): At this stage, cacti were described and classified based on external morphological features. The works of Linnaeus, Jacquin, Pfeiffer, and other scientists laid the foundation for further research.

Schumann's systematics (late nineteenth century): Schumann proposed a more orderly classification system based on a comparative study of morphological characters and flower structure. His work &quot;Gesamtbeschreibung der Kakteen&quot; was an important step forward in the study of cacti.

Britton and Rose's systematics (early 20th century): Britton and Rose conducted extensive field research and studied a wealth of herbarium materials, which allowed them to create a more complete and accurate classification of cacti, especially those growing in the Americas.

Bakeberg's systematics (mid-20th century): Bakeberg, building on the work of his predecessors, proposed his own classification system, which was characterized by greater detail and division of species. His work &quot;Die Cactaceae&quot; became very popular among amateur cactus growers, but was criticized by some professional botanists.

Modern approaches (late XX - early XXI century): Modern methods of research, such as molecular phylogenetics, anatomy, cytology, and others, are now used in cactus taxonomy. These methods make it possible to more accurately establish the relationship between different species and create a more objective classification.It is important to note that cactus taxonomy continues to evolve. As new data is obtained, the classification may change and be refined. Modern research based on molecular data is making significant adjustments to the understanding of the relationships within the Cactaceae family.

The number of species, forms and variations of cacti:

The exact number of cactus species remains a subject of debate and constant refinement, as systematics is a dynamic science. Different sources give different figures, but the following can be said:

Species: It is generally accepted that there are about 1750-1800 species of cacti. However, this number can fluctuate depending on how species boundaries are interpreted and what criteria are used to distinguish them.

Forms and variations: Here the situation is even more complicated. Forms and variations are intraspecific differences caused by genetic characteristics or growing conditions. their number is in the thousands and is almost impossible to accurately account for. One species can have dozens or even hundreds of different forms and variations, differing in size, stem shape, thorns, flower color, and other characteristics.

It is important to understand that the line between species, form, and variation is often blurred. What some taxonomists consider a separate species, others may consider a variation of an already known species. That is why the numbers vary so much.

Introduction to the study

Like a pioneering scientist, I embark on a journey through the world of cacti to uncover the secrets of their habitats, climates, and unique adaptations. These thorny sentinels of deserts, mountains and coastal plains tell stories of survival carved by winds, sun and rare drops of moisture. Let's explore their lives by region and species, and dive into the geography, altitude, and other factors that affect their existence.

North American deserts: Chihuahua and the surrounding area

My expedition begins in the Chihuahuan Desert, which stretches across Mexico and southern Texas. Here, under the scorching sun, I encounter Astrophytum - star-shaped cacti such as Astrophytum myriostigma, with their characteristic ribs resembling stars. Temperatures here can reach +40° C during the day and drop to +10° C at night, with precipitation of less than 200 mm per year. Their light green stems, often without thorns, reflect sunlight, reducing overheating. They grow on rocky slopes where their ribs help collect rare raindrops.

Nearby, on the limestone hills, I find Turbinicarpus - small, almost hidden in the soil cacti such as Turbinicarpus schmiedickeanus. Their range covers the states of San Luis Potosí and Guanajuato, at altitudes of 300 to 3300 meters. The climate here is dry, with hot summers and cool winters, and low humidity, about 20-30%. Their small size and dense thorns protect them from evaporation, and their flowers, which are large compared to their bodies, bloom during short periods of moisture.

Ariocarpus, for example, Ariocarpus fissuratus, occurs in the same areas, but closer to the surface, almost merging with the rocky soil. Their triangular tubercles help to camouflage them, reducing the risk of being eaten by animals. The temperature ranges from +35° C in summer to -5° C in winter, and precipitation is rare, about 150 mm per year. Their deep roots allow them to survive in such conditions, and their large pink flowers are a sign of short-term abundance.

Aztekiums, such as Aztekium ritteri, grow on the gypsum soils of Nuevo León, at an altitude of about 1000 meters. Here, the climate is dry, with temperatures ranging from +25° C to +5° C, and humidity rarely exceeds 25%. Their small, spherical stems with transverse folds are adapted to the high salinity of the soil, and white flowers appear during rare wet periods.

Leuchtenbergia principis, with its long triangular tubercles resembling agave, lives in north-central Mexico, on the limestone hills of Coahuila and Durango. Summers here are hot, up to +35° C, winters are cool, up to 0° C, with precipitation of about 300 mm per year. The deep root and waxy coating help it retain moisture in these conditions.

Pediocactus and Sclerocactus, such as Pediocactus peeblesianus, are found in the southwestern states of the United States and northern Mexico, at elevations of 1500 to 2000 meters. The climate ranges from desert to mountainous, with temperatures ranging from +40° C in summer to -10° C in winter. Their hooked spines collect dew, and their compact forms reduce evaporation.

Echinofossulocactus, if we consider it a part of Sclerocactus, is also adapted to these conditions, with dense spines that protect against the sun and predators, and stems that store water during dry periods.

Cephalocereus, such as Cephalocereus senilis, known as the &quot;Old Man of Mexico&quot; because of its gray pubescence resembling a beard, lives in central and southern Mexico, from the states of Hidalgo to Oaxaca, at altitudes ranging from sea level to 2000 meters. The climate here is subtropical, with distinct wet seasons (June-October) and dry periods when precipitation drops to zero. Temperatures range from +15° C to +35° C, and humidity can reach 40-60%. These columnar giants, sometimes reaching 15 meters, are covered with thick &quot;hair&quot; that protects them from the sun and helps collect moisture from fogs. Their flowers, which bloom at night, attract bats, the main pollinators in the region.

South American regions: Andes and deserts

My expedition continues in South America, where I reach the Andes, home to Rebutia and Gymnocalycium. Rebutia, for example, Rebutia krainziana, grows at altitudes of 2000-4000 meters in Bolivia and Argentina. The climate here is cool, with temperatures ranging from +15° C in summer to -5° C in winter, and humidity is higher, around 50-60%, thanks to fogs. Their small, spherical stems form tight groups to protect them from the cold, and their bright red to yellow flowers bloom during short warm periods. These cacti are true inhabitants of the highlands, their grouping resembles small fortresses against the harsh winds of the Andes.

Gymnocalyciums, such as Gymnocalycium mihanovichii, are found in Argentina, Paraguay, Uruguay and southern Brazil, at altitudes of 500 to 2000 meters. The climate varies from subtropical to temperate, with temperatures ranging from +25° C to +5° C and humidity of 40-70%. Their smooth, often thornless stems and flowers, ranging from white to pink, are adapted to these conditions, and grouping helps them survive in windy mountainous areas. Their variety of shapes - from spherical to slightly elongated - is impressive, and sometimes they even lose their chlorophyll, becoming bright decorative &quot;moon cacti&quot;.

Parodia, for example, Parodia magnifica, lives in the eastern part of South America, from Argentina to southern Brazil and Uruguay, at altitudes of 500 to 1500 meters. The climate here is varied, from subtropical to temperate, with temperatures ranging from +30° C to 0° C, and humidity of 30-60%. Their spherical or columnar stems with dense spines, which can be golden or brown, are adapted to these conditions, and bright yellow flowers appear in the warmer months, adding color to this rocky landscape.

Copiapoa, such as Copiapoa cinerea, grow in the Atacama Desert, Chile and southern Peru, on the coast with elevations ranging from sea level to 1000 meters. It is extremely dry here, with temperatures ranging from +25° C during the day to +10° C at night, with less than 50 mm of precipitation per year, but frequent fogs known as &quot;camanchaca&quot; bring moisture. Their grayish waxy coating reflects sunlight, and their thick spines collect dew, helping them survive in these conditions. These cacti are true masters of survival in one of the driest places on Earth, their shape resembling fossilized balls that merge with the desert.

Islaya, if we consider it part of Eriosyce, occurs in Chile and Peru, on the coast with similar conditions. Their range is from Antofagasta to Arequipa, where the climate is similarly dry, with temperatures ranging from +20° C to +30° C and fogs providing up to 70% of their moisture. Their adaptations, such as collecting fog with their spines and thick cuticles, allow them to survive in these extreme conditions, and their flowers, ranging from yellow to pink, add a rare flash of color to the gray landscape.

Cephalocereus senilis

The bearded man of the desert

Cephalocereus senilis is one of the most interesting cacti known for its unique appearance. Its long white hairs covering the stem resemble a gray beard or thick white hair, which is why it has earned the popular names &quot;Old Cactus&quot;, &quot;Bearded Old Man&quot;, or &quot;Grandfather Cactus&quot;.

Growth environment

Cephalocereus senilis is naturally found in Mexico, mainly in the states of Hidalgo, Querétaro and San Luis Potosí. It grows in dry, rocky areas at an altitude of 1000 to 2000 meters above sea level. Thanks to its thick hair cover, this cactus is well adapted to harsh conditions: the hairs protect it from the scorching sun during the day and the cold at night. In the wild, Cephalocereus can grow up to 12 meters in height, forming large colonies that resemble a forest of &quot;elders&quot;.

Folklore and legends

Local Mexican tribes have long considered this plant sacred. According to legends, bearded cacti are old wise men turned into plants by the gods to guard the desert forever. Some peoples of Mexico called this cactus &quot;Beard of Spirits&quot; and believed that it brought good luck and protected them from evil forces.

There is another interesting legend: if you meet a cephalocereus in the desert and make a wish by touching its hairs, it will definitely come true. It is believed that this is the reason why many travelers and adventurers in Mexico try to find this unique plant.

Flowering is a rare miracle

Although this cactus looks unusual throughout its life, its flowering is a special event. Under natural conditions, cephalocereus begins to bloom only at the age of 10-20 years, and sometimes even later. Its flowers open only at night and are pale pink or white in color, filling the air with a pleasant aroma that attracts pollinating bats.

Growing in culture

Due to its spectacular appearance, Cephalocereus senilis is popular among collectors. It grows well in pots, but needs bright light and dry conditions in winter. To achieve the characteristic thick &quot;beard&quot;, it is recommended to keep it in the sun, because in the shade the hairs can become thinner.

Conclusion.

Cephalocereus senilis is not just a cactus, but a true symbol of desert wisdom. Its unusual appearance, ancient legends and endurance make it a special plant both in nature and in the collections of succulent lovers.
